## Refer out first

Send the user to a clinician for: pain following a fall, dislocation or visible deformity; inability to lift
the arm at all; numbness or weakness running down the arm; night pain that wakes them; or pain that has not
improved over several weeks. Do not attempt to name the condition — shoulder pain has many causes that
present similarly and cannot be distinguished from a typed description.

## Movements to avoid or modify

The shoulder tolerates load well in some positions and poorly in others. When a complaint is active, remove
or modify:

- **Overhead pressing**, particularly behind the neck, which is the least forgiving position of all.
- **Wide-grip flat and decline barbell benching**, where the shoulder sits in extension and rotation under
  heavy load.
- **Upright rows** and heavy lateral raises taken above shoulder height.
- **Deep dips** and any pressing taken to a range that puts the shoulder well behind the torso.
- **Behind-the-neck pulldowns**, and lat pulldowns taken behind the head.
- **End-range stretching under load** across the front of the shoulder.

Severity graduates the response as elsewhere: mild means reduce range and load within the pattern; moderate
means remove the pattern; severe means do not load the shoulder this session.

## Substitutions that keep the session's intent

| Instead of | Consider |
| --- | --- |
| Barbell overhead press | Landmine press, incline press at a shallow angle, neutral-grip dumbbell press within tolerance |
| Wide-grip barbell bench | Neutral-grip dumbbell press, floor press, machine press with a narrower path |
| Dips | Close-grip push-up or push-up on handles, triceps pressdown |
| Behind-neck pulldown | Front lat pulldown, neutral-grip pulldown, chest-supported row |
| Upright row | Face pull, cable rear-delt fly |
| Heavy lateral raise | Lighter lateral raise stopped below shoulder height |

Neutral grips, shallower angles, and reduced range are the three levers. A user who cannot press overhead
usually can still press at a low incline, and a push day survives intact.

## What to train instead

Rotator cuff and scapular work — external rotations at the side, face pulls, prone Y and T raises, serratus
work — is generally tolerated and worth including. Exercise therapy is well supported for rotator-cuff
related shoulder pain, though the specific loading protocol matters less than the fact of loading.

Everything below the shoulder remains available. A shoulder complaint is not a reason to skip legs, trunk
work or conditioning, and saying so keeps the user training.

## Returning to loading

Reintroduce range before load. Press within a pain-free range first, then extend the range, then rebuild the
weight. Expect this to take weeks rather than sessions.

A useful distinction to pass on: mild discomfort during a rehabilitation exercise that settles quickly is
generally acceptable, whereas pain that increases through a set, persists afterwards, or is sharp is a stop
signal. Do not encourage a user to work through the second kind.

## Where the evidence is thinner

- **Diagnostic labels** in this region are unreliable. Clinical tests for specific structures have poor
  agreement, and the terminology has shifted repeatedly — which is exactly why this application should not
  attempt a diagnosis.
- **Scapular position and "dyskinesis"** as a cause of pain is contested; the association with symptoms is
  weaker than the popularity of corrective-exercise programming implies.
- Which **loading protocol** is best for rotator-cuff tendinopathy — heavy slow, eccentric, isometric — is
  unresolved. Reviews support exercise broadly rather than any one variant.
- The claim that specific movements **cause** shoulder impingement is not well established. The
  recommendations above are about what an already-irritable shoulder tolerates, not about which lifts are
  dangerous in general. Do not tell a healthy user to avoid overhead pressing.
