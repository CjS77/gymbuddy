## Count sets per muscle group per week

The unit that matters is hard working sets per muscle group per week, not sets per session and not sets per
exercise. Warm-up sets do not count. A bench press set counts toward chest and, partially, toward shoulders
and arms — which is why a session's real volume for a muscle group is usually higher than the exercise list
suggests.

Rough weekly bands for a muscle group, given the seven groups this application's catalogue defines (Chest,
Back, Shoulders, Arms, Legs, Core, Cardio):

- **Maintenance:** a few hard sets per week will hold what is already there, which is useful during a
  deficit, a busy period, or a block prioritising something else.
- **Productive:** roughly 10 or more hard sets per week for someone actively pursuing size.
- **Beginner:** less than that, and it still works. Beginners respond to almost any structured stimulus, and
  starting near the top of the range wastes the room to progress later.

Arms and Core accumulate substantial indirect volume from pressing, pulling and compound leg work. They
usually need less direct work than users expect, and pushing their direct volume up is a common cause of
elbow and lower-back complaints.

## Frequency

Spreading a muscle group's weekly volume across two or more sessions works better than concentrating it in
one, for both size and strength. The mechanism is mundane: quality degrades within a long session, so the
tenth set of the day is worth less than the third.

The important clause is *at equal weekly volume*. Frequency is a way of distributing volume, not of adding
it. Moving a user from one to three chest sessions a week without changing weekly sets is a reasonable
adjustment; tripling their weekly sets under the banner of higher frequency is not.

Practical consequence for split design: upper/lower and push/pull/legs arrangements hit each muscle group
twice or more per week, which is why they suit most users better than a strict one-body-part-per-day split.
For a user training twice a week, full-body sessions are the only arrangement that achieves it.

## Recovery between sessions

Recovery is what limits how much training can be prescribed. It is not a fixed 48 hours per muscle group;
it varies with the session's volume and intensity, with training experience, and with sleep, stress and
energy availability.

Useful guidance rather than a rule:

- **Heavier, higher-volume sessions need longer.** A limit-strength squat session takes more out of a user
  than a moderate set of leg extensions.
- **Muscle soreness is a poor recovery signal.** It tracks novelty and eccentric loading more than it tracks
  readiness, and it fades as a movement becomes familiar without meaning less training happened.
- **Performance is the good signal.** If load and repetitions are down on a movement across sessions, the
  user is under-recovered or the programme is over-prescribed.
- **Sleep is the highest-leverage recovery variable** the application can mention, and the one users
  routinely trade away.
- **Trained users recover faster** from a given session than untrained ones. Beginners are sore longer and
  should be prescribed less, not more.

Non-training stress counts. A user in a demanding period at work is recovering from that too, and a
programme that ignores it will produce missed sessions read as a discipline problem.

## Where the evidence is thinner

- The **top of the useful volume range** is not established, and high-volume studies are noisy, short, and
  full of individual variation. Any specific "optimal weekly sets" number is an extrapolation.
- **How much indirect volume counts** — whether a row's contribution to biceps volume is a half set or a
  whole one — has no good answer. Count it qualitatively.
- **Recovery timelines** per muscle group are extrapolated from small studies with mostly untrained
  participants and short follow-ups.
- **Recovery modalities** (cold water immersion, compression, stretching, massage) have mixed evidence for
  restoring performance, and cold immersion after resistance training may blunt some adaptations. None of
  them is a substitute for prescribing less.
