## Prescription summary

Hypertrophy is not one of this application's goal kinds — it is a *training emphasis* that serves several of
them. It is the exercise catalogue's most common `purpose` value, it is how a `body_composition` goal builds
or holds lean mass, and it is the accessory work that supports a `strength` goal. Prescribe it when the goal
is size, shape, or lean mass rather than a number on the bar.

- **Repetitions:** 6-12 per working set as the workhorse range; sets from roughly 5 to 30 repetitions all
  build muscle provided they are taken close to failure.
- **Intensity:** moderate, around 65-80% of a one-rep maximum for the workhorse range. Proximity to failure
  matters more here than the load does.
- **Volume:** this is the main lever. Around 10 or more hard sets per muscle group per week is the commonly
  cited target for trained lifters; less than that still works, particularly for beginners.
- **Rest between sets:** 1-3 minutes. Longer than the "short rest for growth" folklore suggests — see below.
- **Frequency:** train each muscle group at least twice a week rather than once, spreading the weekly volume
  rather than adding to it.

## Volume is the dose

Across the dose-response literature, weekly hard sets per muscle group is the variable most consistently
related to how much muscle is gained. That is the single most useful thing to know when writing a session:
if a user's goal is size and they are stalled, the first question is whether the muscle in question is
getting enough weekly sets, not whether the exercise selection is exotic.

Count sets *per muscle group*, not per exercise, and count them across the week rather than the session.
A bench press set is chest volume and triceps volume. Splitting a muscle's weekly sets over two or three
sessions generally works better than piling them into one, which is the practical reason to prefer an
upper/lower or push/pull split over a strict one-body-part-per-day routine for most users.

More is not indefinitely better. The dose-response curve flattens, and past some point extra sets buy
fatigue rather than growth. Where that point sits varies between people and between muscle groups, and the
literature does not pin it down.

## Load, and why the range is so wide

Low loads taken close to failure and heavy loads produce similar muscle growth. This is one of the more
robust findings of the last decade and it has a direct practical consequence: a user with a cranky joint, a
limited set of dumbbells, or no confidence under a heavy bar can still train for size. It is *strength* that
demands heavy loads, not size.

The catch is that low-load sets have to be taken genuinely close to failure to work, which is unpleasant and
easy to under-do. Moderate loads are the pragmatic default because they get close to failure without either
the joint stress of maximal loads or the discomfort of a 30-repetition set.

## Rest between sets

The traditional prescription of 30-60 seconds' rest for hypertrophy, justified by the acute growth hormone
response to short rests, has not held up. Trained lifters resting three minutes gained more muscle and more
strength than those resting one minute in a direct comparison. The mechanism is unglamorous: longer rests
let more total quality volume be performed.

Use 1-3 minutes, biased to the longer end for heavy multi-joint work and the shorter end for isolation work
where fatigue clears quickly. Short rests are a way to buy time in a compressed session, not a growth
stimulus in their own right.

## Proximity to failure

Sets need to be hard. A set stopped four or five repetitions short of failure contributes little. Working
one to three repetitions in reserve on most sets, occasionally to failure on single-joint exercises where
the fatigue cost is low, is the usual compromise.

Whether training *to* failure beats stopping just short of it is genuinely unsettled — the effect on growth
appears small and the fatigue cost is real, particularly on heavy compound lifts where it also degrades the
following sessions.

## Where the evidence is thinner

- The **upper end of useful volume** is not established. Claims of specific optimal weekly set counts are
  extrapolations from a curve that gets very noisy at high volumes.
- **Exercise variety** for "complete development" is widely asserted and weakly evidenced. Varying exercises
  is reasonable for joint comfort and adherence; presenting it as a growth requirement is overclaiming.
- **Time under tension** and specific tempo prescriptions have far less support than the volume and
  proximity-to-failure variables. Do not build a prescription around them.
