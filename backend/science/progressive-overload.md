## The requirement, stated honestly

Training has to get harder over time or it stops producing adaptation, and it has to get harder slowly
enough that tissue keeps up. Both halves are requirements. A programme that never progresses wastes the
user's time; a programme that always progresses eventually hurts them. Everything below is about holding
those two together.

Overload does not only mean more weight. The usable levers, roughly in order of how often they should be
reached for:

1. **More repetitions** at the same load — the default, and self-limiting.
2. **More load**, once the top of the rep range is reached.
3. **More sets**, when the goal is size and recovery allows.
4. **Better execution** — fuller range of motion, more control, less assistance. Real progress that a log
   often misses.
5. **Shorter rests**, for muscular endurance goals only. Never treat this as progression on a strength goal.
6. **More frequency**, the most expensive lever in recovery terms.

## When to add load

Progress on performance, not on the calendar. The NSCA's "2-for-2" rule is a serviceable default: when the
user exceeds the target repetitions by two or more on the final set in two consecutive sessions, increase
the load. This has the property of being self-regulating — a bad week simply does not trigger it.

Increments should be relatively smaller on upper-body and single-joint movements than on lower-body
compounds. The same 5kg jump is a routine step on a squat and a large one on a lateral raise; in relative
terms, roughly 2-5% on upper-body lifts and 5-10% on lower-body lifts is a reasonable band, subject to the
smallest plates available.

Rate of progress depends heavily on training age. A beginner may add load almost every session for the first
months, largely from neural adaptation. An experienced lifter progresses over months, and expecting weekly
increases from them produces a stalled programme and a demoralised user.

## Reading effort

Prescribed load only means something in combination with how hard the set actually was. Repetitions in
reserve — how many more the user could have completed — is the practical measure: 0 RIR is failure, 2 RIR
means two left in the tank.

Rough mapping to the prescriptions elsewhere in this corpus:

- **Strength** work: 1-3 RIR on most working sets. Failure on heavy compounds costs more in fatigue than it
  returns.
- **Hypertrophy** work: 0-3 RIR, biased toward the harder end; single-joint exercises tolerate the
  occasional set to failure.
- **Endurance** work: high repetitions taken genuinely close to failure, since the load is not the stimulus.

Use it to decide the next session. Easy sets across the board mean the load was too light and should go up;
repeatedly hitting failure below the target repetitions means it is too heavy and should come down. Both are
actionable; neither is a judgement of the user.

## Back off, and when

Back off — reduce the load, or stop the exercise — when any of these appear:

- Repetitions fall short of the prescription at a load previously handled, across two or more sessions.
- Technique degrades within a set before the target repetitions are reached.
- The user reports joint pain, as distinct from muscular effort. See the injury documents.
- Sleep, energy or motivation are persistently down alongside stalled performance.

A single bad session is noise — poor sleep, a hard day, a missed meal. Two or three in a row on the same
movement is a signal. Do not deload on the strength of one flat workout.

## Deloads

A deload is a planned reduction — typically holding intensity while cutting volume substantially for a week,
or reducing both — intended to let accumulated fatigue dissipate so that the following block starts fresh.
Common practice places one every four to eight weeks, or when back-off signals accumulate.

Be honest about the evidence here: planned deloads are near-universal in practice and only thinly studied.
The underlying principle — that fatigue accumulates and must be periodically shed — is well founded; the
specific schedule is convention. A deload prescribed by a programme block always overrides ordinary
progression. A designer that adds weight during a deload week has misunderstood the week's purpose.

Reducing volume while holding load is generally preferable to reducing load while holding volume: the heavy
loads maintain the strength while the reduced volume permits recovery.

## Where the evidence is thinner

- **RIR accuracy** is imperfect. Novices underestimate how close to failure they are, and estimates from
  sets far from failure are less reliable than estimates from sets near it. Treat reported effort as
  informative, not exact, and weight it more heavily as the user gains experience.
- **Autoregulation** models have shown mixed results against fixed percentage programming, and the reviews
  point out that the studies are inconsistent in what they even compare. Neither approach dominates.
- **Deload frequency** and structure are practice-derived, as above.
- **Periodisation** models beat non-planned training in the aggregate, but the evidence does not clearly
  favour one model over another for recreational trainees.
