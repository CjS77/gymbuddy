## What a habit goal is

A `habit` goal is denominated in behaviour rather than in performance: "train three times a week", "walk
every day", "stretch after every session". It is measured by whether the thing happened, not by how well it
went. That makes it the one goal kind where the prescription is about the schedule rather than about the
training.

Habit goals deserve to be taken as seriously as performance goals. Adherence is the strongest predictor of
whether any of the other goals are met — a mediocre programme performed consistently beats an optimal one
performed sporadically, and the gap is not close.

## Prescription

- **Set the frequency at what will actually happen**, not at what would be ideal. Three sessions a week
  completed beats five prescribed and two completed, both physiologically and for the user's belief that
  they can do this.
- **Anchor the behaviour to a cue** — a time, a place, an existing routine. Plans of the form "after X, I
  will do Y at Z" are markedly more effective than intentions without a specified when and where.
- **Keep the first sessions comfortably achievable.** Early soreness and early failure are the two main
  reasons people stop. A beginner's first weeks should end with them feeling capable, not wrecked.
- **Reduce friction:** shorter sessions, fewer equipment dependencies, a fixed default session they can fall
  back on when deciding is too much effort.
- **Expect and plan for missed sessions.** A missed session is data about the schedule, not a moral failure.
  A repeatedly missed slot should be moved, not scolded about.

## Timescale

Automaticity takes longer than the folklore suggests. In the most-cited real-world study of habit formation,
the median time for a new daily behaviour to reach its plateau of automaticity was around 66 days, with
enormous variation between individuals and behaviours — some participants took the better part of a year.
The popular "21 days" figure has no basis in this literature.

Two practical consequences: do not treat a two-week streak as consolidated, and do not treat a single lapse
as a reset. In the same work, missing an occasional opportunity did not measurably damage the habit's
formation.

## How habit goals interact with training goals

A habit goal usually *serves* another goal, and when they conflict the habit goal should generally win in
the short term. If a user's programme calls for a 75-minute session and they have 30 minutes, the right
answer is a 30-minute session, not a skipped one: the training loss is small and the adherence loss is not.

Where the habit goal is the user's only goal, keep the prescription within general activity guidelines —
resistance training on two or more days per week, plus regular moderate aerobic activity — and let the
consistency itself be the objective. Adding performance targets to a habit goal that the user did not ask
for is a good way to break the habit.

## Where the evidence is thinner

- Habit-formation research is dominated by **simple daily behaviours** (a piece of fruit at lunch, a short
  walk). A 60-minute gym session with travel and equipment is a considerably more complex behaviour and the
  timescales may not transfer.
- **Streak-based motivation** cuts both ways: it helps some people and makes others abandon the goal
  entirely after breaking a long streak. There is no good evidence for which users are which, so avoid
  making a broken streak feel expensive.
- Effect sizes for individual **behaviour-change techniques** are generally small and highly context
  dependent. Treat the guidance above as sensible defaults rather than a mechanism.
