## Order within a session

Whatever matters most goes first, while the user is fresh. In order:

1. **Warm-up**, general then specific.
2. **The priority movement** — the lift the highest-priority goal is denominated in, or the session's
   heaviest compound.
3. **Remaining compound movements**, heaviest first.
4. **Single-joint and accessory work.**
5. **Conditioning**, if it shares the session and is not the priority.

The rule follows from fatigue, not tradition: performance on the first hard movement is the best it will be
all session, and whatever is scheduled last gets the worst of the user's capacity. When a session serves two
goals, the ordering *is* the priority resolution.

## Warm-up

Warming up improves performance in most studies of it and is cheap. Two components:

- **General:** five to ten minutes of easy whole-body work to raise body temperature.
- **Specific:** the session's first movement performed at ascending loads — a light set, a moderate set, a
  near-working set — before the first working set. This both prepares the tissue and rehearses the movement.

Ascending warm-up sets are more important the heavier the first working set is. A strength session's opening
lift needs several; a set of 15 lateral raises needs none.

Static stretching immediately before heavy or explosive work can transiently reduce force production, though
the effect in typical short durations is small and probably overstated in popular coaching advice. Where
mobility work is needed, dynamic movement beforehand and static stretching afterwards is the low-risk
default. There is no good evidence that pre-session stretching prevents injury.

## Rest intervals by goal

Rest interval is a prescription variable, not a matter of how quickly the user feels ready. The pattern
across goals:

| Emphasis | Rest between working sets | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Maximal strength | 3-5 minutes | The set must be limited by the load, not by prior fatigue. |
| Hypertrophy | 1-3 minutes | Enough recovery to sustain quality volume. |
| Muscular endurance | 30-90 seconds | Incomplete recovery is part of the stimulus. |
| Power / explosive work | 2-5 minutes | Speed collapses under fatigue, and speed is the quality. |

The strength row is the one users most often get wrong, and shortening it measurably reduces the load that
can be sustained across a session. The traditional prescription of very short rests for hypertrophy has not
held up — see the hypertrophy document.

Rest can be shortened between *non-competing* exercises. Alternating a set of rows with a set of leg curls
recovers each while the other works, which is a legitimate way to compress a session under time pressure
without lightening the training.

## Session length

Most productive resistance sessions land between 45 and 75 minutes of work. Longer sessions are usually a
sign of either too many exercises or rest intervals stretching unnoticed.

When time is short, cut exercises rather than load or rest. Three exercises done properly beats six done in
a rush, and a shortened session that happens is worth much more than a full session that does not — see the
habit document.

## Cooling down

Evidence that a cool-down reduces soreness or speeds recovery is weak. A few minutes of easy movement is
harmless and some users like it; do not present it as a requirement, and do not spend prescription budget
on it that could go to training.
