## Beginners are a different prescription

Almost everything else in this corpus describes what works for people who already train. Beginners respond
to far less, respond to almost anything structured, and fail for different reasons — so applying an
experienced lifter's prescription to a novice is both unnecessary and counterproductive.

The first months' gains are largely neural: better recruitment and coordination rather than more tissue.
That is why a beginner adds load almost every session on lifts they are simultaneously still learning.

## Prescription

- **Frequency:** two or three full-body sessions per week. Full-body is the only arrangement that trains
  each muscle group more than once weekly at this frequency.
- **Volume:** low. One to three working sets per exercise, a handful of exercises. Starting near the top of
  anyone's recommended range removes the room to progress and buys unnecessary soreness.
- **Intensity:** moderate — around 60-70% of a one-rep maximum, in the 8-12 repetition range — is enough to
  drive adaptation while the movement is still being learned. Maximal attempts have no place in the first
  months.
- **Exercise selection:** a small set of fundamental patterns — squat, hinge, horizontal push, horizontal
  pull, vertical push, vertical pull, carry or trunk work. Machines and supported variants are entirely
  legitimate here and often preferable while coordination is developing.
- **Progression:** add repetitions, then load, session to session for as long as it keeps working. It will
  keep working for a surprisingly long time, and there is no reason to complicate the programme until it
  stops.

## What actually makes a beginner fail

Not suboptimal programming. In order:

1. **They stop coming.** Everything in the habit document applies with more force here.
2. **The first sessions wreck them.** Severe soreness in week one is a self-inflicted wound from too much
   volume, and it is a common reason people never return for week two. Under-prescribe deliberately at the
   start.
3. **The programme is too complicated** to remember or too dependent on equipment that is busy.
4. **They expect visible change too quickly** and read a normal timescale as failure.

Optimising a beginner's rep ranges is a rounding error against any of these.

## Technique

Movements are being learned, not just performed. Keep loads at a level where technique holds for every
repetition, prefer variants that are hard to do badly, and accept slower loading in exchange for a pattern
that will carry heavy weight later.

This application cannot see the user, which is a real limit and worth being honest about. It can prescribe
conservative loads and simple movements; it cannot correct a lift it has not watched. Suggesting a few
sessions with a coach for the fundamental lifts is reasonable advice, not an admission of failure.

## Setting expectations

Strength moves first and fastest — noticeable within weeks. Visible changes in size or composition take
months and depend as much on nutrition as on training. Endurance improves quickly at first from a low base.
Saying this up front converts a discouraging plateau into an expected one.

## Where the evidence is thinner

- The boundary between **beginner, intermediate and advanced** is conventional rather than measured, and
  users progress out of the beginner response at different rates. Judge by whether session-to-session
  progression still works, not by elapsed time.
- **Machines versus free weights** for novices is largely a preference and availability question; the
  evidence does not strongly favour either for general strength and size outcomes.
- Claims about a **"newbie gains" window** of a specific duration are folklore. The response tapers
  gradually and individually.
