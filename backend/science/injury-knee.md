## Refer out first

Send the user to a clinician for: a knee that locks, catches or gives way; significant swelling, especially
if it came on rapidly after an incident; inability to bear weight; obvious deformity; or pain following a
twisting injury or impact. Persistent pain that has not improved over several weeks also warrants
assessment.

As with the shoulder, do not attempt to name the problem. Anterior knee pain, patellar tendon pain and
meniscal irritation are managed differently and cannot be told apart from a typed description.

## Movements to avoid or modify

Knee complaints are usually provoked by the combination of deep knee flexion and load, or by high volumes of
repetitive impact. When one is active, remove or modify:

- **Deep squatting under load** — full-depth back and front squats, and deep leg press.
- **High-impact plyometrics** — jump squats, box jumps, depth jumps, bounding.
- **Running volume**, particularly downhill running and sudden increases in weekly distance.
- **Loaded lunging and step-ups** through a large range, and Bulgarian split squats at depth.
- **Open-chain knee extension at heavy loads** through full range, which loads the patellofemoral joint
  hardest in the deeper part of the range.
- **Deep, rapid change of direction** in any conditioning work.

Reduced range is the first lever here, more so than at other joints: many users who cannot squat to depth
without pain can squat to a box at a comfortable height without any.

## Substitutions that keep the session's intent

| Instead of | Consider |
| --- | --- |
| Full-depth back squat | Box squat or partial squat to a pain-free depth, belt squat |
| Deep leg press | Leg press through a shortened range |
| Walking lunge | Split squat with reduced depth, step-up to a low box |
| Heavy leg extension | Lighter leg extension through a pain-free range, or isometric holds |
| Running | Cycling, elliptical, swimming, rowing, incline walking |
| Jump training | Non-impact power work, or omit for this block |

Posterior-chain work — Romanian deadlifts, hip thrusts, glute bridges, hamstring curls, back extensions —
is usually well tolerated when knee flexion under load is not, and keeps a leg day meaningful.

## What to train instead

Hip and quadriceps strengthening is the best-supported intervention for the most common cause of anterior
knee pain in active people, so the answer is generally *more* targeted strength work rather than rest.
Hip abduction and external rotation work alongside quadriceps loading within a pain-free range is the
combination the consensus guidance points to.

Tendon-related knee pain responds to progressive loading rather than to avoidance; isometric holds are often
tolerated when dynamic work is not, and give a way to keep loading while symptoms settle. Complete rest
tends to leave the tendon less capable when training resumes.

## Returning to loading

Rebuild range before load, and load before impact. A reasonable order is: pain-free partial range at light
load, then progressively deeper range, then heavier load at that range, then a gradual return to running or
jumping if the user's goals need it.

For impact work specifically, increase weekly volume gradually. Sudden spikes in running distance are the
common precipitant, and a return to the previous weekly mileage in one step is the mistake to head off.

Some discomfort during rehabilitation loading that settles quickly is generally acceptable; pain that builds
through a set, persists into the next day, or is sharp means back off.

## Where the evidence is thinner

- The claim that **deep squatting damages knees** is not supported for healthy, trained users with adequate
  technique and sensible loading. The restrictions above apply to a knee that is currently symptomatic. Do
  not tell a healthy user to avoid squatting deep.
- **Knee position over the toes** as a rule to avoid is a long-standing coaching cue with weak support; some
  forward travel of the knee is normal and unavoidable in most squatting patterns.
- Which specific **exercise protocol** works best for patellar tendinopathy is unresolved — heavy slow
  resistance, eccentric loading and isometrics all have support, and the consensus statements recommend
  progressive loading without crowning a method.
- **Running and knee osteoarthritis**: recreational running is not associated with increased risk in the
  available evidence, contrary to common belief. This is worth saying to a user who has stopped running out
  of that fear.
