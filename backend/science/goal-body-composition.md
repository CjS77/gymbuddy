## Two goal kinds, one prescription

`bodyweight` goals track the number on the scale. `body_composition` goals track what that weight is made
of — body fat percentage, waist measurement, lean mass. The training prescription is nearly identical; the
difference is in how progress is read, so both goal kinds are covered here.

Direction matters more than kind. A `bodyweight` goal can run in either direction: losing fat and gaining
muscle are opposite energy-balance problems that happen to share a training prescription.

## The honest framing

Training does not drive fat loss; energy balance does, and the largest term in energy balance is what is
eaten. Exercise volumes large enough to produce weight loss on their own are larger than most people will
sustain. What resistance training reliably does during a deficit is protect the lean mass that would
otherwise be lost alongside the fat — which is the difference between ending up smaller and ending up
leaner.

This is worth saying plainly to a user rather than implying that the right workout will do it. A programme
that promises fat loss from training alone sets up a failure that the user will read as their own.

This application does not track food intake and should not prescribe a diet. It can say that a deficit is
required, that protein intake matters, and that a slower rate of loss preserves more lean mass — and then
stay in its lane.

## Prescription

- **Resistance training is the anchor.** Train for hypertrophy (see that document): moderate loads, 6-12
  repetitions, each muscle group at least twice a week. During a deficit, hold intensity roughly where it
  was and let volume drop if recovery suffers — dropping the load is what loses the muscle.
- **Compound movements first.** Squat, hinge, press, row, carry. They cover more muscle per unit of session
  time, which matters when session time is the constraint.
- **Cardio is additive, not a substitute.** Add it to the resistance work rather than replacing it, and
  prefer modalities that do not compete with the lifting for recovery — walking, easy cycling, incline
  treadmill. The general health baseline is around 150 minutes per week; meaningful weight-loss support
  takes substantially more than that, which is why diet does the heavy lifting.
- **Rate of change:** roughly 0.5-1% of bodyweight per week when losing. Faster deficits cost lean mass and
  performance. Gaining is slower still — muscle accrues at a small fraction of the rate fat does.
- **Protein:** around 1.6 g per kg of bodyweight per day is the commonly cited point past which additional
  protein adds little for muscle gain; requirements sit at the higher end during a deficit.

## Reading the numbers honestly

Scale weight is noisy. Day-to-day swings of a kilogram or more come from water, gut contents, and glycogen
— particularly in the first two weeks of any dietary change, where a rapid initial drop is mostly water and
a plateau in week three is not a stalled metabolism. Judge trend over at least two to three weeks, never
session to session.

For `body_composition` goals specifically: consumer body-fat estimates from impedance scales are imprecise
and drift with hydration. A tape measure at a fixed landmark, taken under consistent conditions, is often
the more trustworthy signal. Treat any single body-fat reading as approximate.

Weight can also move the wrong way for the right reason. A user gaining muscle while losing fat may see a
flat scale for weeks; if their lifts are climbing and their measurements are falling, the programme is
working and the `bodyweight` goal is the wrong instrument.

## Where the evidence is thinner

- Static equations converting an energy deficit into predicted weight loss **overestimate** real-world loss,
  because expenditure adapts as weight falls. Do not project a linear trajectory from a deficit figure.
- Specific claims about **fasted cardio, meal timing, and fat-burning zones** producing better fat loss than
  matched-energy alternatives are not well supported.
- The **exact** rate of loss that preserves lean mass depends on how lean someone already is; a heavier
  person tolerates a proportionally larger deficit than a lean athlete.
- Simultaneously gaining muscle and losing fat is most achievable for beginners, people returning after a
  layoff, and those carrying more body fat. For a lean, trained user it is slow and unreliable.
