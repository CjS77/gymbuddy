## Refer out first

Before any of the training guidance below, screen for the features that need a clinician rather than a
programme: numbness, tingling or weakness running down a leg; pain following a significant fall or impact;
unexplained weight loss, fever, or pain that wakes the user at night. Loss of bladder or bowel control, or
numbness in the saddle region, is a medical emergency — say so plainly and stop there.

Most lower-back pain has no identifiable structural cause and improves. That is genuinely reassuring
information and worth giving, because fear of the back being fragile is itself associated with worse
outcomes. It is not a reason to skip the screen.

## Movements to avoid or modify

When a lower-back complaint is active, the patterns that load the lumbar spine hardest are the ones to pull
out of the session:

- **Loaded spinal flexion under load** — conventional and stiff-leg deadlifts, good mornings, bent-over
  rows, heavy kettlebell swings.
- **Axial compression** — back squats, standing overhead press, heavy loaded carries.
- **End-range flexion** with any load — sit-ups, weighted crunches, toe-touch machines, Roman-chair
  hyperextensions taken to end range.
- **Rotation under load** — Russian twists, cable woodchops, heavy rotational throws.

The graduation of the response follows severity. Mild: reduce load and range, keep the pattern. Moderate:
remove the pattern, train around it. Severe: do not load the spine at all this session.

## Substitutions that keep the session's intent

The point is to keep training, not to cancel the day.

| Instead of | Consider |
| --- | --- |
| Back squat | Leg press, split squat, hack squat, belt squat |
| Conventional deadlift | Hip thrust, glute bridge, back-extension through pain-free range |
| Bent-over barbell row | Chest-supported row, seated cable row, single-arm supported dumbbell row |
| Standing overhead press | Seated press with back support, landmine press |
| Sit-ups, weighted crunches | Dead bug, bird dog, side plank, Pallof press |
| Farmer's carry (heavy) | Lighter carries over shorter distances, if pain-free |

Chest-supported and seated variants are the general answer: they train the same muscles with the trunk's
load taken by a bench or a machine.

## What to train instead

Isometric trunk work — plank, side plank, bird dog, dead bug — loads the trunk musculature without moving
the spine through range, and is the usual starting point when the back is irritable. Add anti-rotation work
such as the Pallof press when it is tolerated.

Beyond that, train everything that does not provoke it. Upper body with the trunk supported, single-leg work
within tolerance, and easy walking are all usually available.

## Returning to loading

Reintroduce the removed patterns gradually and unloaded first: pattern with bodyweight, then light load,
then rebuild. Expect to start well below the previous working load — usually a substantial reduction — and
progress by repetitions before load.

Movement, generally, beats rest. Prolonged bed rest is associated with worse outcomes than staying active
within tolerance, and exercise therapy is one of the better-supported interventions for chronic low back
pain. The user should be moving; the question is what they are moving *with*.

## Where the evidence is thinner

- **Which** exercise approach works best for back pain is not settled. The Cochrane evidence supports
  exercise over no exercise, with modest effect sizes; it does not crown a specific method, and confident
  claims that one protocol is *the* answer outrun the data.
- The relationship between **lifting technique and back injury** is more contested than gym culture
  suggests. Spinal flexion under load is not inherently injurious for everyone, and the "never round your
  back" instruction is a simplification. For a user whose back is currently sore, avoiding it is still the
  sensible default.
- **Core strength as injury prevention** has mixed evidence. Trunk work is worth prescribing for capacity
  and for symptom relief; framing it as injury-proofing overstates what is known.
- Imaging findings such as disc bulges are **common in people with no pain at all**, so a user's scan result
  is not a reliable guide to what they can train. That conversation belongs with their clinician.
