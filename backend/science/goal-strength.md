## Prescription summary

A `strength` goal is a goal to raise the load a specific movement can be performed with. The training that
does this is heavy, low-repetition, well-rested, and practised often enough that the movement itself gets
better — strength is partly a skill, not only a tissue property.

- **Repetitions:** 1-6 per working set for a trained lifter; 6-8 for a beginner still learning the movement.
- **Intensity:** roughly 80-90% of a one-rep maximum for most working sets, occasionally above. A beginner
  gets stronger on far less — 60-70% is enough in the first months, because the early gains are neural.
- **Volume:** 3-6 working sets per movement per session; several *hard* sets per week per lift matter more
  than a precise total. Multiple sets beat single sets for trained lifters.
- **Rest between sets:** 3-5 minutes. This is not padding. The set is meant to be limited by the target
  muscles, not by residual fatigue from the previous set.
- **Frequency:** 2-3 sessions per week touching the movement or a close variant. Frequency is how the skill
  gets rehearsed.

## Why heavy and why rested

Strength expressed on a specific lift depends on force the muscle can produce *and* on the nervous system's
ability to recruit and coordinate it under load. Heavy sets train both; light sets taken to failure train
the first far better than the second. This is the clearest asymmetry in the literature: low-load training
can build comparable muscle size to high-load training when sets are taken close to failure, but high-load
training produces greater maximal strength. If the goal is a number on the bar, the bar has to be heavy.

Long rests exist for the same reason. A set cut short by lactate and breathlessness from the previous set is
not a heavy set, it is a moderate set that feels heavy. Restricting rest to 60-90 seconds measurably reduces
the load that can be sustained across a session.

## Exercise selection

Prioritise the movement the goal is denominated in. A goal to bench 100kg is trained by benching; accessory
work supports it but does not substitute for it. Order the session so the goal lift is performed first,
while fresh.

Multi-joint movements (squat, deadlift, bench press, overhead press, row, weighted pull-up) carry the load
this kind of training needs. Single-joint work has a place as accessory volume, but a strength block built
from isolation exercises will not deliver.

## Progression

Add load when the top of the prescribed rep range is exceeded with good technique, not on a fixed calendar.
The NSCA's "2-for-2" heuristic is a reasonable default: when the lifter clears two repetitions above the
target on the last set in two consecutive sessions, increase the load. Increments should be relatively
smaller on upper-body and single-joint lifts than on lower-body compounds — the same absolute jump is a much
larger relative jump on a press than on a squat.

Progress on a strength goal slows the longer someone has trained. A beginner may add load weekly; an
experienced lifter may add it monthly, and needs planned variation in intensity and volume to keep moving.

## Where the evidence is thinner

- The **exact** dose-response for strength is contested. Meta-analyses agree that more than one set beats one
  set and that heavy loads beat light ones, but they disagree on the shape of the curve past a moderate
  weekly volume, and individual response varies a lot.
- **Minimum effective dose** work suggests trained lifters can maintain and even slowly improve 1RM on
  surprisingly little volume. Read that as reassurance for a busy user, not as a reason to prescribe minimal
  training to someone who wants to get strong.
- **Training to failure** on heavy compounds is not required for strength and adds fatigue that costs the
  next session. Leave a repetition or two in reserve on most working sets.
