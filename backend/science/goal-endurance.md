## Two different things called endurance

An `endurance` goal in this application can mean either of two things, and they are trained differently.
Read the goal's exercise before prescribing.

- **Muscular endurance** — repeated submaximal contractions of a specific muscle. "50 press-ups", "a
  two-minute plank", "20 pull-ups". Trained with resistance work at high repetitions and short rests.
- **Cardiorespiratory endurance** — sustained whole-body work. "Run 10km", "cycle for an hour". Trained with
  continuous or interval aerobic work, and governed by weekly duration far more than by session design.

Most confusion in endurance programming comes from applying one prescription to the other kind of goal.

## Muscular endurance prescription

- **Repetitions:** 15 or more per set, often far more for bodyweight movements.
- **Intensity:** below roughly 60% of a one-rep maximum; for bodyweight exercises the load is whatever it is
  and the variable is repetitions.
- **Volume:** 2-4 sets per exercise, with the total repetitions per session as the meaningful number.
- **Rest between sets:** short — 30-90 seconds. Here, unlike hypertrophy training, incomplete recovery *is*
  part of the stimulus, because tolerating accumulated fatigue is the adaptation being sought.
- **Frequency:** 2-4 times per week. High-repetition low-load work is comparatively easy to recover from.

Sets must still approach a meaningful level of effort. Three easy sets of twenty when thirty are possible
trains very little.

## Cardiorespiratory endurance prescription

- **Weekly volume** is the primary variable: the general health baseline is roughly 150 minutes of moderate
  activity per week, or about 75 minutes of vigorous activity, and endurance *goals* usually need more than
  the baseline.
- **Intensity distribution:** most sessions easy enough to hold a conversation, with one or two harder
  sessions per week. Making every session moderately hard is the most common self-coaching error — too hard
  to recover from, too easy to drive adaptation.
- **Interval work** raises maximal aerobic capacity effectively and in less time than continuous work, which
  makes it valuable for time-constrained users. It is also more fatiguing and more injury-prone; it
  supplements easy volume rather than replacing it.
- **Progression:** increase duration before intensity, and increase weekly volume gradually. Large sudden
  jumps in running volume in particular are associated with overuse injury.

## Endurance alongside resistance training

Schedule the priority quality first, or on its own day. If the user's endurance goal is the priority, heavy
leg work the day before a key run will compromise it; if strength is the priority, see the competing-goals
document — long, hard aerobic sessions interfere with strength and size gains in the same muscles.

Low-intensity aerobic work interferes least. A user pursuing strength who also wants general fitness should
be steered toward easy cycling or walking rather than hard running.

## Where the evidence is thinner

- The **10% per week** rule for increasing running volume is a widely repeated heuristic without strong
  supporting evidence. The underlying principle — increase gradually, avoid sudden spikes — is sound; the
  specific number is not a finding.
- Composite **training-load ratios** proposed to predict injury risk have been substantially criticised on
  methodological grounds. Do not present any such ratio to the user as a risk score.
- The optimal **intensity distribution** for recreational athletes is extrapolated largely from elite
  endurance populations, where mostly-easy training is well documented.
