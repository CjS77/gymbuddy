## Do not average competing goals

When a user holds several goals at once, the failure mode is to blend the prescriptions into a middle that
serves none of them. Three sets of ten at a moderate weight with 90 seconds' rest is not a compromise
between a strength goal and an endurance goal; it is a hypertrophy session that neither goal asked for.

Goals are resolved by **priority**, not by averaging. The highest-priority goal sets the session's core
prescription — its rep range, its intensity, its rest intervals, and the exercise that goes first while the
user is fresh. Lower-priority goals are served by what is left: accessory work, the back half of the
session, or different days of the week.

State the resolution explicitly. A user who wants to squat 140kg and run a half marathon should be told
which one this block is prioritising and what that costs the other. Silently splitting the difference is
how eight weeks get spent moving neither number.

## Which pairs actually conflict

Not every combination is a conflict. Sort them before applying priority.

- **Compatible.** `habit` with anything — a consistency goal is served by any session that happens.
  `strength` with `hypertrophy` emphasis — overlapping prescriptions, easily sequenced heavy-first.
  `body_composition` with `hypertrophy` — the resistance work is the same work.
- **Mildly conflicting.** `strength` with `endurance` when the endurance work is low intensity, in a
  different modality, and on separate days. Manageable with scheduling.
- **Genuinely conflicting.** `strength` or size goals with a `bodyweight` goal running downward: building
  tissue needs an energy surplus and losing weight needs a deficit. Also `strength` with high-volume,
  high-intensity `endurance` in the same muscles.

## Concurrent strength and endurance

Training strength and endurance simultaneously blunts strength and size gains relative to training strength
alone. The effect has been observed repeatedly since the original work in 1980 and is not controversial in
direction, though its magnitude varies with how the aerobic work is arranged.

Interference gets worse with the aerobic work's frequency, duration, and intensity, and it is greatest when
the same muscles are involved — running interferes with squatting far more than cycling interferes with
pressing. Endurance adaptations, notably, are much less impaired by concurrent lifting than the reverse.
The traffic is mostly one-way.

Practical mitigations, in rough order of effect:

1. **Separate the sessions.** Different days where possible; at minimum, several hours apart.
2. **Priority quality first**, when they must share a session. Whatever comes second is compromised.
3. **Choose a non-competing modality.** Cycling or rowing rather than running for a lifter.
4. **Cap the hard aerobic work** rather than the easy volume. Long slow work interferes least.

## Strength and fat loss

The other unavoidable conflict. In a deficit, expect to *maintain* strength rather than build it, and treat
maintenance as the win it is. Keep loads heavy and reduce volume rather than the reverse — training light
during a deficit is the reliable way to lose muscle alongside the fat. Protein at the higher end of the
usual recommendations helps preserve lean mass.

If the user genuinely wants both and will not sequence them, be explicit that progress on both will be
slower than progress on either alone. Beginners and returning trainees are the exception; they can often do
both at once for a while.

## Sequencing beats compromise

The best answer to two conflicting goals is usually time, not balance: a block prioritising one, then a
block prioritising the other. Six weeks of focused strength work followed by six weeks of focused
conditioning will outperform twelve weeks of half of each, and it gives the user a clear story about what
each block is for.

Where a target date forces the goals to be concurrent, say what the trade-off is and let the priority
ordering carry the decision.

## Where the evidence is thinner

- The **magnitude** of the interference effect varies widely between studies, and much of the underlying
  research uses training programmes more demanding than a typical recreational user's. Treat direction as
  well established and size as uncertain; a user doing two easy runs a week alongside lifting is unlikely to
  notice it at all.
- The proposed **molecular mechanisms** for interference are contested, and mostly irrelevant to
  programming. The scheduling advice above does not depend on them.
- **Optimal block lengths** for sequencing are conventional practice rather than a finding.
